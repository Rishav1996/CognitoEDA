Understanding Diabetes Risk Factors

Prioritized Risk Factor Identification for Targeted Interventions

Our analysis clearly identifies Glucose, BMI, Age, and Pregnancies as the most significant and consistently impactful predictors of diabetes. These factors show strong statistical differences between diabetic and non-diabetic groups and robust correlations with diabetes presence, making them primary drivers of diabetes risk.

Key Factors:

  • Glucose: p-value 1.2e-39, correlation 0.4758 (highly significant)
  • BMI: p-value 9.7e-18, correlation 0.3097 (highly significant)
  • Age: p-value 1.1e-17, correlation 0.3090 (highly significant)
  • Pregnancies: p-value 3.7e-08, correlation 0.1987 (highly significant)

Strategic Focus on Genetic and Familial Risk Assessment

The 'DiabetesPedigreeFunction' feature consistently emerges as a significant predictor, highlighting the crucial role of genetic predisposition and family history in diabetes susceptibility. Understanding this allows for more personalized and preventative health strategies.

Key Factors:

  • DiabetesPedigreeFunction: Statistically significant p-values across tests (Mann-Whitney U: 1.19e-06, Logistic Regression: 3.70e-06, Kolmogorov-Smirnov: 6.84e-05) and moderate Spearman correlation (0.1754).

Differentiated Approach for Ancillary Risk Factors: Insulin and Blood Pressure

While most features are strong individual predictors, 'Insulin' and especially 'BloodPressure' show a more nuanced or less direct individual predictive relationship with diabetes outcome. Insulin's role appears complex, significant in some models but not all. BloodPressure consistently acts as a weaker individual predictor, suggesting it might be more of a co-morbidity indicator or part of a broader metabolic syndrome, rather than a primary singular driver of diabetes onset for screening.

Key Factors:

  • Insulin: Mann-Whitney U p-value (0.065 - above 0.05 threshold), Logistic Regression p-value (0.0004 - significant), Spearman correlation (0.0665 - weak).
  • BloodPressure: Logistic Regression p-value (00.073 - not statistically significant at alpha=0.05), Spearman correlation (0.1429 - relatively weak), despite significant Mann-Whitney U and KS-test results suggesting distributional differences.
